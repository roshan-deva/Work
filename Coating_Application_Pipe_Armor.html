The Case Briefing

Bare steel pipe rusts. Buried in the ground or run through a corrosive process line, unprotected steel is on borrowed time. Coating is the pipe's armor — a set of layers bonded onto the steel that keep water, salts, and oxygen from ever touching bare metal again.

This mill runs two coating lines: External (the outside of the pipe, facing the soil) and Internal (the inside, facing whatever flows through it). Same mission, different armor.

3LPE / 3LPPFBE / DFBEInternal liquid paint

Why bother armoring at all?

Coating can't be perfect forever — the source material is upfront about this: "some degree of coating failure is unavoidable." That's why every buried pipeline also runs Cathodic Protection (CP) — a low-level electrical system that keeps protecting the steel even at the handful of spots where the coating eventually lets go. A good coating plus CP is the real armor; either one alone isn't enough for a pipe meant to last decades underground.

Coatings usually fail for one of six reasons: the wrong coating was picked for the job, it was applied badly, it got physically damaged while laying the pipe, the material itself failed early, the cathodic protection wasn't enough to back it up, or it simply aged out. Final inspection exists to catch the "applied badly" failures before the pipe ever leaves the yard.

1. Prep the Steel "Nothing sticks to dirt, rust, or a damp surface." ▸

Stations: receiving inspection → water wash → gas-oven preheat → abrasive blasting → air blow-out → blasted-surface inspection.

  • Water wash: up to 100 Bar, water as hot as 70°C — strips loose dust, mud, and soluble salts before anything else happens.
  • Preheat: radiant gas burners raise the pipe to about 85°C. Purpose is simple — dry the surface completely, since blasting a damp pipe just wastes the blast.
  • Abrasive blasting: steel grit fired at the surface at 80 m/s, twice, to reach a rough "anchor pattern" (50-110 microns) that coating can physically grip — a perfectly smooth pipe is actually harder to coat well than a properly roughened one.
  • Inspection gate: visual check of clean finish, roughness, salt residue, and dust level. Fail this and the pipe loops all the way back to Receiving Inspection — not just back one step — for a full re-process (marked "NTC" on the line).
2. Prime the Steel "Chemistry, then heat — the surface gets ready to bond." ▸

Stations: phosphoric acid + high-pressure water wash → two rounds of induction preheating → final induction heating.

  • Phosphoric acid wash: not a coating — a chemical handshake. It reacts with any leftover chlorides and salts on the steel, breaking their bond with the surface so the rinse water actually carries them away instead of trapping them under the coating.
  • Induction heating: two heater sets bring the pipe up to about 250°C (tuned to whatever the FBE powder needs). Hot steel is what actually melts and fuses the epoxy powder on contact in the next stage — the heat lives in the pipe, not in an oven around it.
3. Apply the Armor "Powder that melts into the steel, then a tough outer shell." ▸

Stations: FBE/epoxy powder spray booth → adhesive + polyethylene/polypropylene extrusion → weld-seam coating.

  • Epoxy spray: up to 60 electrostatic spray guns coat the hot pipe with fusion-bonded epoxy powder, which melts on contact into a thin, tightly bonded primer layer (up to 600 microns single, 1600 dual).
  • Adhesive + PE/PP: a second machine extrudes a thick outer jacket over the epoxy — this is the layer doing the mechanical heavy lifting (impact, abrasion, handling damage) that epoxy alone is too thin to survive.
  • Weld-seam coating: laser sensors find the raised weld bead and a fast hydraulic plunger deposits extra molten HDPE right there — the seam would otherwise end up under-thickness compared to the rest of the pipe body.

Fig. — Electrostatic spray guns applying FBE powder. The mist is charged powder finding its way to the grounded, hot pipe.

4. Check and Ship "Cool it, measure it, shock it, mark it, move it." ▸

Stations: 40m quenching zone → visual & dimensional inspection → offline lab testing → holiday detection & repair → cutback cleaning → final inspection → marking → stacking & dispatch.

  • Quenching: chilled water cools the coating solid without leaving water marks — laminar flow troughs and controlled nozzles do the work.
  • Coating thickness gauge: a digital eddy-current tester checks the epoxy layer, the total system thickness, and gets checked itself against calibration foils every shift.
  • Holiday detector: runs an electrode over the coating at a set voltage (about 5V per micron of thickness for FBE, or up to 25kV for 3LPE) — any pinhole completes a circuit through to the bare steel and trips an alarm, both visible and audible.
  • Final inspection: the last gate — checks for the defect catalog (see the Defect Gallery below), cutback length and cleanliness, bevel shape, then marks and clears the pipe for dispatch.

1. Prep, Inside Out "Same blast-and-inspect idea, aimed down a pipe's throat." ▸

Stations: receiving inspection → preheat (2 stations) → abrasive blasting (2 stations) → blow-out → blasted-surface inspection.

  • Preheat, different target: external preheat is about drying the surface before blasting; internal preheat is tuned to a "Dew Point Condition" — hot air is whirled inside the pipe until the internal surface sits about 3°C above the atmosphere's dew point, so moisture physically can't condense back onto it. Same goal as external (a dry surface), different way of proving it's met.
  • Blasting: same anchor-pattern goal as external, just via impellers and blast booms sized for reaching inside a pipe rather than spraying an outer surface.

Did you know? This line's NTC (re-process) loop works exactly like external's — a failed blasted-surface inspection sends the pipe all the way back to Receiving Inspection, not just one step back.

2. Coat and Finish "A spinning pipe and a lance that reaches where spray guns can't." ▸

Stations: cutback tape masking → internal coating application → tape removal → visual inspection → stenciling and paint curing at the yard.

  • Cutback masking: tape protects the pipe ends (the part that'll later be welded to the next joint) from getting coated at all.
  • Internal coating application: liquid paint, not powder this time — an "unsupported painting lance" reaches deep into the pipe while it spins at high RPM. The lance gets the material where a spray gun's straight-line reach never could, and the spin does the even-coverage work a robot arm would otherwise need to do. A plural-feed hot-airless system mixes the two paint components on the fly (ratios from 1:1 to 10:1, held within ±1%) and heats the paint for easy flow, covering anywhere from 50 to 2000 microns thick.
  • Tape removal & finish: pull the masking tape, visually check the result, then stencil and let it cure at the yard.

Defect Gallery

What final inspection is actually hunting for — two real examples, from the source material.

Fig. — Holidays (pinholes). Each dark dot is a spot the coating never sealed — exactly what a holiday detector's alarm is built to catch.

Fig. — Disbonding. The coating has come loose from the steel in wrinkled lines — it's still physically present, just no longer doing its job underneath.

Final inspection's checklist covers more than these two:

  • Fish eyes / craters: small circular flaws in the coating surface, usually from contamination during application.
  • Blistering: raised bubbles — often trapped moisture or gas pushing the coating up from underneath.
  • Sagging / drips: coating that ran before it set, leaving uneven thickness (a 3LPE/3LPP-specific watch-out).

Did you know? A pipe can pass every one of these checks and still eventually see some coating failure in the ground — that's precisely why Cathodic Protection exists as the backup, not a sign the inspection failed.
